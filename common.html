Doctrine of Precedent: Also known as stare decisis, it means decisions of higher courts are binding on lower courts, establishing a system of legal precedent. Helps in consistency and predictability in legal interpretations In summary, the Doctrine of Precedent is a cornerstone of the common law system, promoting consistency, fairness, and stability in legal decision-making by emphasizing the importance of prior judicial decisions.

Judicial Review: Courts have the power of judicial review to examine and strike down laws or government actions that are deemed unconstitutional. Derived from common law principles, this power safeguards individual rights.

Natural Justice: Encompasses principles of fairness and justice in legal proceedings. Involves the right to be heard, unbiased decision-making, and adherence to the principles of audi alteram partem (hear the other side).

Torts: The law of torts, largely based on common law principles, addresses civil wrongs and liabilities. Covers areas like negligence, defamation, and trespass, providing remedies for individuals who have suffered harm.

Contract Law: Principles of contract law, derived from common law, govern the formation, performance, and enforcement of contracts. The Indian Contract Act, 1872, is influenced by common law principles. Key elements of contract law include: Enforceability, Consent,Certainty and Possibility of Performance,Legality of Purpose,Legal Capacity,Consideration,Intention to Create Legal Relations,Offer and Acceptance.
